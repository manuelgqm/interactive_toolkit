Step 4: Risk management

Decision-making process involving considerations of political, social, economic, and technical factors with relevant risk assessment information relating to a hazard so as to develop, analyze, and compare regulatory and non-regulatory options and to select and implement the optimal decisions and actions for safety from that hazard.
Source:Duffus&Worth IUPAC©.

The risks associated with a potential for harm due to exposure to chemicals have to be identified, assessed and managed appropriately. The distinction between assessment and management of risks is a key issue.

Risk assessment procedure is designed to evaluate, usually quantitatively, the nature and magnitude of a potential risk. But on its own, risk assessment has limited value.
Risk management on the other hand, is the decision-making process to accept a known or assessed risk and/or the implementation of actions to reduce the consequences or probabilities of such an occurrence.

When developing risk management decision-making strategies, two complementary approaches are considered, usually in sequence:

  • effects-oriented policies: effects on human health and the environment; and
  • source-oriented policies: prevention of effects by controlling releases.

The effects of a chemical on health and the environment via an exposure pathway, for example, represents the first important parameter. Then suitable exposure standards can be developed. These standards are then translated into a source-related policy to control the releases of the chemical to ensure that exposure standards are not exceeded. Risk management therefore considers both policies.

In more general terms, risk management decision-making should embody a systematic, and structured approach to chemical risks, that allows the parties involved to:

  • identify risks/problems that need to be eliminated or reduced to evaluate;
  • identify ways in which these risks can be eliminated or, ‘managed’ to control; and;
  • decide upon the most appropriate strategy to achieve reduction of risk to implement and monitor

The risk management process can also be described as comprising a five-step process, ranging from identification of the problem to implementation and monitoring control actions. The process is an iterative one and not a linear sequence of actions. The five steps have been recognized as an important cyclical process to follow so that governments can make informed decisions on priority chemicals.

Risk assessment is an essential tool for preventing health and environmental damages.
An adequate risk assessment must take into account the following factors

  • Location of chemical
  • Chemical properties
  • Environmental conditions necessary for the storage, use, disposal, etc.
  • Sector (where and how it is used)
Develop a risk reduction strategy for all possible uses of the chemical, and along the cycle of use, from production, through industrial and public use, to recycling and disposal.
Consult with stakeholders on risk reduction measures intended to establish the management plan taking into account their needs and priorities.

Key stakeholders: Industries, trade unions, consumer organizations, NGOs, and administrations involved.

Defining risk reduction measures:
All stakeholders must ensure a proper management of chemicals at workplaces in terms of their:

  • Storage
  • Management
  • Classification of chemicals by hazards
  • Proper labelling
  • Training to all individuals who come through chemistry
  • Plan of emergency related to chemical risk

Planning toxic use reduction implies:
• A use reduction policy
• An assessment of how and how much is used and how much toxic waste is generated
• A list of economically viable, technical abatement options
• An implementation plan

The substitution of hazardous chemicals should be considered a priority measure whenever possible

The implementation of the risk reduction plan must be based on assessment reports and stakeholders' view.

This gives a way to most appropriate strategy formulation in order to reduce the risk. Decision makers will enforce the plan and carry out continual monitoring to evaluate its efectiveness at national level.

Some International organizations have developed useful tools to assist governments and industry to manage risks posed by chemicals:

ILO – International Chemical control tool-kit: The International Chemical Control Toolkit outlines a scheme for protection against harmful and dangerous chemicals in the workplace. It is designed for small and medium sized enterprises (SME, s) in developing countries.

  • GIZ “Practical Chemicals Management Toolkit”: hands-on tool for the implementation of practices and a system for the resource-efficient management of chemicals and wastes along the lines of the Strategic Approach to International Chemicals Management (SAICM).

GTZ Chemical Management Guide for Small and Medium Sized Enterprises (Germany): Improve Chemical Management to Gain Cost Savings, Reduce Hazards and Improve Safety
